Introduction to Neural Networks

May 15, 2024 By Vaibhav Kesharwani

Neural networks form the backbone of modern artificial intelligence systems. These computational models are inspired by the structure and function of the human brain, consisting of interconnected nodes (neurons) that process and transmit information.

What are Neural Networks?

At their core, neural networks are a series of algorithms designed to recognize patterns. They interpret sensory data through a kind of machine perception, labeling or clustering raw input. The patterns they recognize are numerical, contained in vectors, into which all real-world data, be it images, sound, text, or time series, must be translated.

Neural networks help us cluster and classify data. You can think of them as a clustering and classification layer on top of structured data. They help group unlabeled data according to similarities among example inputs, and classify data when they have a labeled dataset to train on.

Key Components of Neural Networks

1. Neurons

The basic unit of computation in a neural network is the neuron, a mathematical function that takes one or more inputs and produces an output. The output is typically a nonlinear transformation of a weighted sum of the inputs.

2. Architecture

Neural networks have different layers of neurons:

  • Input Layer: Receives the initial data
  • Hidden Layers: Performs computations and transfers information from input to output
  • Output Layer: Produces the final result

Figure 1: Typical architecture of a neural network

3. Weights and Biases

The connections between neurons have associated weights that determine the strength of influence one neuron has on another. Each neuron also has a bias term that allows the activation function to be shifted.

How Neural Networks Learn

Neural networks learn through a process called training, which involves:

1. Forward Propagation

Input data is passed through the network, layer by layer, until it reaches the output layer. Each neuron applies its activation function to the weighted sum of its inputs.

2. Loss Calculation

The difference between the predicted output and the actual target is measured using a loss function.

3. Backpropagation

The error is propagated backward through the network, and the weights are adjusted to minimize this error.

4. Optimization

An optimization algorithm, such as gradient descent, is used to update the weights in a way that reduces the loss.

Applications of Neural Networks

Neural networks have revolutionized many fields and are applied in various domains:

  • Image and speech recognition
  • Natural language processing
  • Medical diagnosis
  • Financial forecasting
  • Autonomous vehicles
  • Recommendation systems

As computing power continues to grow and algorithms become more sophisticated, neural networks are expected to play an increasingly important role in solving complex problems and advancing artificial intelligence.
